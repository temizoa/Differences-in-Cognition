Introduction

The University of Maastricht has come to us to help them gain a deeper understanding of the Five-Point test that they have administered to roughly 1500 young individuals via computer-based assessments.

They have collected data on each participants performance during the test. This data consists of for example: their clicked selections, timing of each click, and time spent on each answer. The data also has results from participants CITO-score and Big-5 Personality test.

The aim here is to move beyond the traditional outcome measure of the test, which is the total number of correct answers completed in any given time frame. While this does give some insight, it has significant limitations in further analyzing and assessing ones performance.

This is where our main objective grows from. We as a project group want to focus on what happens in a more nuanced form during the test itself. We want to identify patterns, such as the sequence of figures the participant chooses or maybe changes in speed, based on factors like figure relationships or errors made during the test.

These patterns can lead to new scoring metrics, and with that hopefully bring more insight to assist psychologists in better understanding the test-takers cognitive process.

Getting a better idea about this is important as it can uncover potential challenges a young individual might face and show where they may need more assistance (e.g, school).

Next to a better understanding, we also hope to bring new metrics that can bring in additional insight. For example, metrics that make it possible to analyze if there is a possible correlation between strategies used and certain personality traits of the young individual.

The CITO test

The CITO test is an official test given in The Netherlands, usually at the end of primary school. It is used to help teachers in determining how well students understand foundational courses, directing their academic path at high school, and providing additional information on how they may develop as learners.

The test assesses certain subjects like language, math, and methods of learning. There are multiple areas to this test, each focusing on a different area of expertise and knowledge. The questions are created to span a wide range of difficulty levels, ensuring an in-depth awareness of a students cognitive abilities.

The test has a multiple-choice structure. In order to ensure consistency and fairness for every test-taker, it is conducted under carefully monitored circumstances.

The final score is between 500 and 550. It is usually divided by 5 steps of 20%, where each step has a certain comparison to the countrys mean.

Its important to note that the test score is not the definite decider in which direction the student goes starting high school, it is intended as a guideline for the teachers who should take all factors into consideration in their final assessment.

The Five-Point test.

The Five-Point test is among other areas a problem-solving and creativity assessment that involves connecting five dots arranged like in the shape of the number 5 on a set of dice. The goal is to evaluate a participants ability to strategize, think critically, and show creative problem solving skills within a given time frame.

In this context, the participant is given 180 seconds to create as many unique figures or shapes as they can by connecting dots to create straight lines between the 2 points theyve selected.

As was mentioned earlier, the goal is to use the test as a measure of problem-solving skills, strategic thinking, and creativity. It helps assess a participants ability to see patterns, think outside the box, and create multiple solutions whilst under time pressure.

The Big-Five Personality test.

The Big-Five Personality test, also known as the Five-Factor Model is one of the few psychological frameworks that are used by various doctors, researchers among some examples. It is widely considered the most scientifically validated. It looks into five major dimensions, hence its name.

Openness looks at creativity, a willingness to explore and chase after new ideas. If you score high; you are often more likely to be curious, imaginative and open to the idea of change.

Conscientiousness looks at organization, responsibility and reliability in your behaviour. If you score high here you are more likely to be organized and diligent in your pursuits in all kinds of areas.

Extraversion looks at how outgoing, sociable, and energized you are by social interactions. If you score high you are more likely to thrive in social settings, enjoy the company of others and often naturally take on leadership roles.

Agreeableness looks at your level of warmth, cooperation, and compassion. If you score high on Agreeableness you tend to be more considerate, empathetic, and cooperative.

The final trait Neuroticism looks at your emotional stability and resilience to stress. If you score high on Neuroticism you are more likely to experience heightened emotional reactivity and could possibly be more prone to anxiety and mood swings.

It can be a valuable tool for a more nuanced comprehension of individuals differences.

Fixing the row ordering issue

In a normal case, the order of rows with the certain variables will be:

  1. patternsm
  2. timestampsm
  3. statuscl
  4. numbercl
  5. timestampcl
  6. timestampload

But, sometimes in the dataset, the ordering will be duplicated which has nothing to do with each other, so it will appear like:

  1. patternsm
  2. patternsm
  3. timestampsm
  4. timestampsm
  5. statuscl
  6. statuscl
  7. numbercl
  8. numbercl
  9. timestampcl
  10. timestampcl
  11. timestampload
  12. timestampload

The following code will fix that

What this does, is that it looks through each row with a predefined order as we have mentioned above. If the order is obstructed it will put the first "faulty" item in a stack. Because of the way (and luck) that the dataset either has a perfect order or one that is duplicated, we don't have to account for any other factors except those 2 combinations.

it will then look in the list of stack items and put those in the correct order so the whole dataset has the same order.

Data understanding

To begin understanding the data, we will do some basic aggregation and we will extract time periods of a test that was taken.

We make 18 time window blocks of 10 seconds each, the test is 180 seconds long so that divides the windows perfectly.

In[]:
# Helper functions
def convert_to_list(s):
    try:
        return literal_eval(f'[{s}]') if s else []
    except (ValueError, SyntaxError):
        return []

def format_time(seconds):
    if np.isnan(seconds) or seconds < 60:
        return f"{seconds:.2f} sec"
    else:
        minutes = int(seconds // 60)
        seconds = seconds % 60
        return f"{minutes} min {seconds:.2f} sec"

# Constants
MAX_GAP = 300  # 5 minutes in seconds
BLOCK_DURATION = 10  # Block duration in seconds
TOTAL_BLOCKS = 18  # Total number of blocks

# Load the dataset
file_path = 'fivepoint_test_processed.csv' 
df = pd.read_csv(file_path, delimiter=',', header=0)

# Process each student's data
student_data_list = []
for student_id, student_group in df.groupby('idll_vo3lv'):
    # Extract and process timestamps and patterns for the student
    student_timestamps = student_group['timestampcl'] 
    student_patterns = student_group['patternsm'] 

    # Flatten the lists and sort timestamps
    timestamps = sorted([ts for sublist in student_timestamps.apply(convert_to_list) for ts in sublist])
    patterns = [pattern for sublist in student_patterns.apply(convert_to_list) for pattern in sublist]

    # Convert timestamps to datetime objects
    timestamps = [datetime.fromtimestamp(ts / 1000) for ts in timestamps]

    # Initialize blocks with both clicks and unique patterns
    if timestamps:
        start_time = timestamps[0]
        blocks = [{'start': start_time + timedelta(seconds=i * BLOCK_DURATION),
                   'end': start_time + timedelta(seconds=(i + 1) * BLOCK_DURATION),
                   'clicks': 0,
                   'unique_patterns': set()} for i in range(TOTAL_BLOCKS)]

        # Process timestamps and patterns
        for ts, pattern in zip(timestamps, patterns):
            pattern_key = tuple([pattern]) if isinstance(pattern, int) else tuple(pattern)
            for block in blocks:
                if block['start'] <= ts < block['end']:
                    block['clicks'] += 1
                    block['unique_patterns'].add(pattern_key)

        # Calculate total duration and average time between clicks
        valid_time_diffs = [((timestamps[i] - timestamps[i-1]).total_seconds()) for i in range(1, len(timestamps))
                            if (timestamps[i] - timestamps[i-1]).total_seconds() <= MAX_GAP]
        test_duration = (timestamps[-1] - timestamps[0]).total_seconds()
        average_time_between_clicks = np.mean(valid_time_diffs) if valid_time_diffs else 0

        # Prepare student data with block information
        student_data = {
            'Student_ID': student_id,
            'Total_Clicks': len(timestamps),
            'Total_Unique_Figures': len(set(pattern_key for pattern_key in patterns)),
            'Average_Time_Between_Clicks': format_time(average_time_between_clicks),
            'Test_Duration': format_time(test_duration)
        }
        # Add block-wise clicks and unique pattern counts to the student data
        for i, block in enumerate(blocks, 1):
            student_data[f'Block_{i}_Clicks'] = block['clicks']
            student_data[f'Block_{i}_Unique_Patterns'] = len(block['unique_patterns'])

        student_data_list.append(student_data)

# Create the DataFrame with the aggregated data
analysis_df = pd.DataFrame(student_data_list)

Preprocessing

The elbow method

The elbow method is used to determine the optimal number of clusters (K) for K-Means clustering. It helps strike a balance between cluster quality and complexity by identifying the point where adding more clusters doesn't significantly reduce within-cluster variance (WCSS). The elbow point in the WCSS plot is chosen as the optimal K.

In[]:
from sklearn.cluster import KMeans
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler

# Corrected function to convert time in 'min sec' format to seconds
def convert_time_to_seconds(t):
    if isinstance(t, str):  # Check if the input is a string
        if 'min' in t:
            minutes, seconds = t.replace(' sec', '').split(' min ')
            return float(minutes) * 60 + float(seconds)
        else:
            return float(t.replace(' sec', ''))
    else:
        return t  # Return the input if it's not a string

# Applying the corrected function to the DataFrame

The ANOVA (Analysis of Variance) test is used in our project to determine if there are statistically significant differences in the 'Test_Duration' means across the different clusters identified in the FPT (Five Point Test) data. By comparing the average test durations between clusters, we can assess whether the observed differences are likely due to random chance or if they reflect a true variation in test-taking behavior that could be related to underlying factors. such as cognitive abilities, test strategies, or other characteristics captured by the clusters. The F-statistic from the ANOVA test measures the degree of variation between the group means, and the p-value tells us whether this variation is statistically significant
